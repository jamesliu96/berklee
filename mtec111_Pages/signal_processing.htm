In a general sense, we could say that any time audio is converted from acoustic energy into electrical energy, sound is being processed. Any device in the audio chain, from a microphone to amplifiers and speakers, alters or colors the original sound in some way. Practically though, we'll limit our discussion to devices intended to manipulate sound in some way, either to creatively alter a signal or to correct a problem.

Effects Categories

Dynamics processors

Dynamics processors affect the loudness of a sound over time, and help us correct levels, increase the sustain of decaying sounds, and filter out background noise. Follow the link for a more detailed explanation.

  • Compression - Reduces the dynamic range of signal. Main Controls:
    • Threshold - The level at which the circuit becomes active.
    • Ratio - Ratio between input and output. e.g.: in a 4:1 ratio, an increase of 8-dB results in a 2-dB increase in signal.
  • Limiting - A type of compression used in recording sources that have high transients. Generally the ratio is around 10:1.
  • Expansion - Increases the dynamic range. Increases the gain of loud signals and reduces the gain of quiet signals. Used for noise reduction.
  • Gate - Prevents signals below a certain amplitude from passing.

Spectrum Processors

Spectrum processors are filters or equalizers used to alter the harmonic content of a signal. These processors affect the tone color of a sound and allow us to equalize audio, create special tone-color effects like wah-wah, and help us create clarity in a mix. Generally a filter will boost or cut selected frequencies by an amount expressed in dB. The range of frequencies effected is referred to as bandwidth or "Q." Bandwidth is usually expressed in octaves. Follow the link for a more detailed explanation.

Types Include:

  • Pass Band Filters - Usually found in synthesizers.
    • Low Pass - Allows frequencies below the cutoff to pass. Reduces high frequencies.
    • High Pass - Allows frequencies above the cutoff to pass. Reduces low frequencies.
  • Shelving Filter - Reduces all frequencies above or below cutoff by a certain amount.
  • Parametric - Boosts or cuts frequencies within a selectable bandwidth.
  • Graphic - Divides the audio spectrum into equally spaced center frequencies. e.g.: Single Octave Graphic EQ: divides audible spectrum into 11 bands an octave apart.

20 Hz, 40 Hz, 80 Hz, 160Hz, 320Hz, 640Hz, 1.25kHz, 2.5kHz, 5kHz, 10kHz, 20kHz.

Time-based processors

These processors repeat the signal over time and create a variety of musical effects such as reverb, chorus, flanging, echo, slap-back delay, etc… Follow the link for a more detailed explanation.

  • Modulation Effects - These effects have very short delay times that continuously change.
    • Flange - Very short (2-20 ms) delay which causes phase cancellation.
    • Chorus/Doubling - Short delay (15-40ms) used to make a signal sound thicker or richer. Chorusing is often accomplished by using subtle changes in pitch that change continuously.
  • Delay Effects - Signal is delayed. Repeats are discreet events usually defined by the length of delay and the number of repeats.
    • Slapback/Echo - Audible delay(s) of the original signal.
  • Reverb - Artificially creates a sense of space using closely spaced random echoes. No repeats are heard. Reverb is usually described by:
    • Type of space, as well as its size e.g.: Room, Hall. etc. Often defined by frequency response.
    • Decay -The amount of time a sound continues to be heard.
    • Early Reflection is the amount of time before reverb is heard.
  • Pitch Shifting - Pitch is tuned up or down in either musical steps or cents.

Processor Architecture

  • Dedicated Effect - A device which does only one type of effect. Generally higher end devices designed to perform particularly well.
  • Multi Effects - A device which can process more than one effect simultaneously
    • Serial Effects
    • Parallel Effects
